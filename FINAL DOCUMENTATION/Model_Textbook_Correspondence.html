1 Scope and anchoring

The tool is a single progressive teaching application, not four separate models. One engine runs throughout; a stage selector unlocks blocks in sequence, and two internal gates decide what that engine actually computes at each step. Everything below — the IS reduced form, the policy rate, the Phillips curve, the exchange rate — is the same code; the stages simply switch parts of it on and off.

1.1 The two gates that drive every stage

The whole progression is governed by exactly two switches in the engine. Understanding them makes every stage transition below predictable rather than arbitrary:

  • The open/closed gate (openOn(), true when the current stage's economy is open). When open, the exchange-rate channel is live: the net-export terms x₁Y* − n₁(ε−1) are added to the IS equation and the UIP panel is active. When closed, those trade terms are dropped and the exchange-rate machinery is inert. This gate oscillates across the four stages — off, on, off, on — which is why the exchange rate appears, disappears, and returns.
  • The inflation gate (effectivePiE(), true once the Phillips-curve block is unlocked). It decides whether the model uses expected inflation at all. While locked, the engine computes the real rate as r = i — Blanchard's fixed-price short run, where nominal and real rates coincide. Once unlocked, it computes r = i − πᵉ and the Fisher wedge opens. Unlike the open/closed gate, this one latches: it turns on at stage 3 and stays on.

The four stages are just the four combinations of these two gates that the syllabus walks through. Note the order follows the teaching sequence, not Blanchard's chapter order: the exchange rate (Ch. 17–19) is introduced before inflation (Ch. 9) because the open-economy IS is a natural second step from the closed IS, whereas the Phillips curve is a larger conceptual jump. This is deliberate and is not a fidelity error.

1.2 The four stages

UnlockedGoods market (the Y = C + I + G equilibrium and multiplier) and the LM / policy-rate line.
EngineBoth gates off. No trade terms, no inflation: the IS equation is the closed reduced form and the real rate equals the nominal rate. This is Blanchard's Chapter 5 short-run IS-LM verbatim.

The starting point: a closed economy with fixed prices. Output is determined by the goods market and the central bank's rate; there is no exchange rate and no inflation. Because prices are fixed, r = i and the distinction between nominal and real rates has not yet appeared. At the baseline rate the goods market clears below potential (Y ≈ 90 < Yₙ = 100) — a genuine short-run result of r = i, not a calibration quirk (see §4.1).

UnlocksThe exchange-rate channel: uncovered interest parity (UIP) and net exports.
EngineOpen/closed gate flips on; inflation gate still off. Trade terms x₁Y* − n₁(ε−1) enter the IS equation and the UIP panel activates. Prices remain fixed, so r = i still holds.

The economy opens. The interest rate now also drives the exchange rate through UIP (a higher domestic rate appreciates the currency), and the exchange rate feeds back into output through net exports. This is the open-economy IS of Chapters 18–19. Inflation is still absent — the point of this stage is to isolate the exchange-rate mechanism before adding price dynamics on top of it. The open multiplier is smaller than the closed one (import leakage), so the IS curve is steeper here than in stage 1.

Prices come alive. The model returns to a closed economy so the student can study inflation and the nominal–real rate distinction without the exchange rate as a confound. This is the stage where the engine's real-rate calculation genuinely changes: expected inflation now drives a wedge between the nominal rate the central bank sets and the real rate that determines output, and the Phillips curve links the output gap to inflation. With the real rate free to fall below the nominal rate, output can now reach potential (Y returns to 100). This is Blanchard's Chapter 9 IS-LM-PC model. UIP is switched off here deliberately: it returns, unchanged, at stage 4.

Stage 4Full model (IS-LM-PC-UIP)  ·  open economy  ·  r = i − πᵉ  (in-app label: "Full Model")

EngineOpen/closed gate flips back on while the inflation gate stays latched. Every block is live at once: trade terms in the IS equation, UIP active, r = i − πᵉ, and the Phillips curve running.

The complete open-economy model with inflation. The exchange rate returns — but now it interacts with the Phillips curve, so a policy change moves the real rate, the exchange rate, net exports, output, and inflation together. This is the synthesis Blanchard never presents in a single chapter: it combines the open-economy IS and UIP of Chapters 18–19 with the Phillips curve of Chapter 9. It is documented here as an explicit synthesis rather than transcribed from one place in the text, which is exactly why the block-by-block correspondence in the rest of this document matters — each piece is anchored to its own source even though the assembled model has no single textbook home.

The block-by-block correspondence follows. §2 derives the closed-economy IS block from Blanchard's building blocks; §3 collects its coefficients and multipliers; §4 is the numeric check-case; §5 the LM/MP block; §6 the Phillips curve; §7 the open-economy exchange-rate and foreign-output channel.

2 The Closed-Economy IS Equation (reduced form)

Y = [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁)] · [c₀ + d₀ + G − c₁ T − d₂ r]

Derivation from Blanchard's building blocks

Each term of the reduced form above is one of Blanchard's standard building blocks. The subsections below take them in turn — consumption, investment, the real interest rate, and the equilibrium condition — then §3 collects the resulting coefficients.

2.1 Consumption — Chapter 3

Y = [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁)] · [c₀ + d₀ + G − c₁ T − d₂ r]

ToolC = c₀ + c₁(Y − T)

Blanchard's consumption function is the linear relation

C = c₀ + c₁ Y_D
(eq. 3.2)

with disposable income defined by the identity Y_D ≡ Y − T, so that

C = c₀ + c₁ (Y − T)
(eq. 3.3)

c₁ is the propensity to consume, restricted to 0 < c₁ < 1 (eq. 3.2 discussion). Blanchard's empirical estimate for the United States is around 0.6, with the Appendix 3 regressions yielding 0.5 and 0.8 (Ch. 3, “The size of the multiplier”).

Chapter 3 takes investment as exogenous (I = Ī, eq. 3.4) purely as a simplification, explicitly flagged as temporary: Blanchard notes a firm facing higher sales “might well decide they need more machines” and promises a richer treatment in Chapter 5. I = I(Y, i)

2.4 Equilibrium condition — Chapter 3 / Chapter 9

Y = [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁)] · [c₀ + d₀ + G − c₁ T − d₂ r]

ToolY = C + I + G
BlanchardY = C + I + G  (eq. 3.1 / 9.1)
StatusEXACT

Goods-market equilibrium requires production equal demand:

Y = Z
(eq. 3.6)

Substituting consumption, investment, and government spending gives Blanchard's short-run output equation, eq. (9.1):

Y = C(Y − T) + I(Y, r + x) + G
(eq. 9.1)

Substitute the linear forms into the equilibrium condition Y = Z (eq. 3.6). The risk premium is not part of the model, so x = 0 throughout:

Y = c₀ + c₁(Y − T) + d₀ + d₁ Y − d₂ r + G

Collect the terms in Y on the left (this is Blanchard's own “move c₁Y to the left, reorganise the right” step from the Ch. 3 derivation of eq. 3.8):

Y − c₁ Y − d₁ Y = c₀ + d₀ + G − c₁ T − d₂ r

(1 − c₁ − d₁) Y = [c₀ + d₀ + G − c₁ T − d₂ r]

Divide through by (1 − c₁ − d₁):

Y = [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁)] · [c₀ + d₀ + G − c₁ T − d₂ r]
(tool reduced form)

This is the accelerator-augmented version of Blanchard's eq. (3.8). The multiplier 1/(1 − c₁ − d₁) generalises his 1/(1 − c₁): the induced-spending propensity is now the sum c₁ + d₁, because both consumption and investment respond to output.

3.2 Why the tax multiplier is asymmetric

The tax multiplier −c₁/(1 − c₁ − d₁) carries d₁ in the denominator but only c₁ in the numerator. This is not an inconsistency. A tax change acts on disposable income, and in this model only consumption responds to disposable income; investment responds to output (Y), not to (Y − T). So the direct first-round impact of a tax rise runs through consumption alone (numerator −c₁), while the subsequent amplification runs through both consumption and the investment accelerator as output falls (shared denominator 1 − c₁ − d₁).

By contrast a spending change hits demand directly and fully (numerator 1), which is why |∂Y/∂T| < ∂Y/∂G and the balanced-budget multiplier is non-zero.

3.3 The c₁ slider

Because every IS coefficient is a function of c₁, the tool exposes c₁ as a live slider (a behavioural primitive). Moving c₁ with d₁ held fixed recomputes the multiplier, the G coefficient, the T coefficient, and the r coefficient simultaneously.

  • Range 0.3–0.8 (covering Blanchard's Appendix 3 estimates of 0.5 and 0.8, centred near his point estimate of 0.6).
  • As c₁ + d₁ → 1 the multiplier 1/(1 − c₁ − d₁) diverges. The slider is capped at c₁ = 0.8 (with d₁ = 0.1, keeping c₁ + d₁ = 0.9 short of 1), so the divergent region is simply unreachable — the cap is the safeguard, there is no separate instability warning. This connects to Blanchard's Ch. 3 “trick question” about c₁ > 1.
  • The “Show the equations behind this chart” panel beneath the IS-MP diagram displays the consumption, investment, and government terms with their live values, and the summed output line, so the reduced-form algebra is visible while dragging.

4.1 The closed economy in the unified tool (Lower output pre-PC)

The closed economy is not a separate engine: it is the open-economy IS with the open-only terms switched off when the UIP block is locked — the import leakage m is forced to 0 and the trade terms (x₁·Y* − n₁·(ε − ε_base)) are dropped. With m=0 the denominator returns the closed multiplier k = 1/(1 − c₁ − d₁) = 2.5 (c₁=0.5, d₁=0.1). The open/closed gate of §1.1 is applied in one place — the IS calculation itself — so the computed equilibrium, the plotted IS curve, its label, and its drag handle all switch to the closed form together; nothing has to be toggled block by block.

The Role of Inflation on Output: In the pre-PC stage (the short run), prices are fixed and there is no inflation. Because there is no inflation, the real interest rate is equal to the full nominal rate (r = i = 3%). This higher real rate makes borrowing more expensive, which lowers investment and keeps output below potential (at Y = 90).

Once the Phillips Curve unlocks (the medium run), the economy experiences a positive inflation rate of 2%. This positive inflation mathematically keeps the real interest rate lower than the nominal rate (r = 3% − 2% = 1%). This lower real rate directly spurs higher investment, which in turn boosts the overall output of the economy back up to its potential (Yₙ = 100).

The neutral nominal rate (the rate that holds output at potential when inflation is on target) follows Blanchard's Ch. 23 definition of the target nominal rate:

ī = rₙ + π
(neutral nominal rate, Ch. 23)

The neutral nominal rate is the baseline rate the Central Bank sets when the economy is perfectly balanced (output is at potential and inflation is at target). When the Taylor rule is active, the Central Bank sets its actual target interest rate by adjusting up or down from this neutral baseline based on the current inflation and output gaps.

5.3 The unemployment–output-gap conversion (shared with the PC block)

Blanchard's policy rule and his Phillips curve are written in unemployment, but the tool works in output. The conversion is a single identity derived in Chapter 9 (the steps that take eq. 9.2 to eq. 9.3); it is recorded once here and referenced by both the Taylor rule below and the Phillips-curve block.

By definition the unemployment rate is unemployment over the labour force:

u ≡ U/L = (L − N)/L = 1 − N/L
→ N = L(1 − u)

Maintaining the Chapter 7 simplification that output equals employment (Y = N):

Y = N = L(1 − u)
(eq. 7.1)
and at the natural rate Yₙ = L(1 − uₙ)

Subtracting gives the deviation of output from potential:

Y − Yₙ = L[(1 − u) − (1 − uₙ)] = − L (u − uₙ)

Equivalently, the conversion identity used throughout the tool:

(u − uₙ) = − (Y − Yₙ) / L
(from eqs. 9.2–9.3)

Two consequences follow whenever a Blanchard relation in u is rewritten in Y: a sign flip (u and Y move oppositely) and a division by L. In Blanchard's one-good setup Y ≈ N ≈ L, so dividing by L and normalising by Yₙ nearly coincide; the tool normalises by Yₙ. This is the same reconciliation the Phillips-curve block requires (eq. 9.3 carries the factor α/L).

5.5 Interest smoothing (a tested departure from the textbook rule)

Tooli = ρ·i₋₁ + (1 − ρ)·(ī + φ(π − π) + ψ(Y − Yₙ)/Yₙ),   ρ = 0.75

StatusTOOL ADDITION
ChangePartial adjustment toward the rule-implied target each period. Blanchard's stated rule sets the rate outright; smoothing is a standard real-world extension retained here for numerical stability (see below).

The tool includes interest-rate smoothing (ρ = 0.75), i.e. the policy rate partially adjusts toward its rule-implied target each period: iₜ = ρ·iₜ₋₁ + (1−ρ)·i. This is a standard real-world extension but is not part of Blanchard's stated Ch. 23 rule, which has no lagged-rate term. It is retained deliberately to ensure model stability: without this gradual adjustment, a central bank reacting instantly to current inflation and output gaps can trigger severe period-to-period interest rate oscillations that would destabilise the interactive tool. This smoothing acts as a vital anchor for the engine's dynamics, making it a necessary and intentional departure from the textbook rule.

6 The Phillips-curve block (inflation)

π = πᵉ + (α / Yₙ)(Y − Yₙ) + shock

Block 3. Blanchard derives the Phillips curve from the labour-market model (Chapter 7) in Chapter 8, then converts it to output space in Chapter 9. The tool follows this chain. The conversion from unemployment to output uses the identity already derived in §5.3.

6.1 From wage- and price-setting to inflation — Chapter 8

Wage setting (eq. 7.1) with Blanchard's specific functional form F(u, z) = 1 − αu + z:

W = Pᵉ · (1 − αu + z)
(eq. 7.1, specific form)

Price setting (eq. 7.3): firms set price as a markup over the wage:

P = (1 + m) · W
(eq. 7.3)

Substituting the wage into the price equation gives the price level in terms of the expected price level, the markup, the wage-push factor, and unemployment:

P = Pᵉ (1 + m)(1 − αu + z)
(eq. 8.1)

Rewritten in inflation terms (derivation in Blanchard's chapter appendix), with time indexes:

πₜ = πᵉₜ + (m + z) − α uₜ
(eqs. 8.2 / 8.3)

The two supply-side terms are distinct objects in Blanchard: m is the markup of prices over wages (market power / degree of competition), and z is everything in wage-setting other than unemployment (union bargaining power, unemployment-benefit generosity, employment protection). The tool exposes them as two separate controls accordingly.

6.2 The natural rate and the Phillips curve — eqs. 8.4–8.5

π = πᵉ + (α / Yₙ)(Y − Yₙ) + shock

Tooluₙ = (m + z_struct)/α_WS ;   Yₙ = L(1 − uₙ)
Blancharduₙ = (m + z)/α  (eq. 8.4)
StatusEXACT
Noteα_WS and z_struct are the engine's code names for Blanchard's α and z in this formula. α_WS is a fixed structural constant (3.0), not the adjustable Phillips-curve slope — the two are kept separate; see §6.5. z_struct is the structural wage-push term, distinct from the transitory shock added to the Phillips curve (§6.7).

The natural rate of unemployment is the rate at which actual inflation equals expected inflation. Setting πₜ = πᵉₜ in eq. (8.3) and solving:

uₙ = (m + z) / α
(eq. 8.4)

This is the key cross-block link: m and z move the natural rate, and therefore potential output. A rise in either raises uₙ and so lowers Yₙ = L(1 − uₙ). They are substitutes (eq. 8.4 depends on m + z), so a markup shock can be offset by a fall in z — e.g. a reduction in union bargaining power restoring potential output. This is a genuine medium-run rebalancing (Yₙ is restored), not a temporary cancellation of an inflation blip.

Substituting eq. (8.4) back into eq. (8.3) gives the Phillips curve in unemployment space:

πₜ − πᵉₜ = − α (uₜ − uₙ)
(eq. 8.5)

πₜ − πᵉₜ = −α · [−(Y − Yₙ)/L] = (α/L)(Y − Yₙ)
(eq. 9.3)

Blanchard's output-space slope is therefore α/L. The tool normalises the output gap by potential output Yₙ rather than the labour force L, writing the slope as α/Yₙ. Since Yₙ = L(1 − uₙ), the two normalisations differ only by the factor (1 − uₙ) ≈ 0.95 — a unit conversion, chosen for consistency with the IS and MP blocks (which all use Yₙ = 100), not a change of substance.

The value of the tool's α is a different matter. In Blanchard, the α in this equation is the same α as in the natural-rate formula (eq. 8.4). In the tool it deliberately is not: the natural rate uses the fixed α_WS = 3.0, while the Phillips-curve slope is its own slider (default 0.3) — a factor of ten apart, not 5%. Why the model cannot use one number for both is explained in §6.5; it is the single deliberate deviation in this block.

6.4 Distinguishing tool variables from textbook parameters

The interactive nature of the tool requires slightly different notation from the textbook to distinguish structural parameters (which determine the natural rate) from transitory shocks (which hit the economy at a given natural rate). The tool uses:

  • α_WS: Blanchard's α — the structural sensitivity of wages to unemployment in the WS relation. Fixed in the engine at 3.0 so that uₙ = (m + z_struct)/α_WS gives a plausible natural rate (5% at baseline). This is the only α that enters uₙ and Yₙ.
  • α: The Phillips-curve slope slider (default 0.3, range 0.05–0.5). It enters only the PC equation. Moving it never changes uₙ or Yₙ — that separation from α_WS is deliberate; see §6.5.
  • z_struct (or z): The structural wage-push factor (e.g. employment protection, minimum wage, unemployment benefits) that sits permanently in the WS relation. A change in z_struct permanently shifts the natural rate of unemployment uₙ and therefore potential output Yₙ.
  • shock: A transitory, independent cost-push shock (e.g. a sudden oil price spike or supply chain disruption) that shifts the Phillips curve directly without altering the underlying structural labour-market balance (the WS-PS crossing). It fades over time and does not change uₙ or Yₙ.

Both z_struct and shock push prices up, but through different mechanisms with different persistence, so the tool separates them. z_struct is a permanent wage-setting pressure (higher bargaining power, benefits, or minimum wages) that raises the natural rate and moves Yₙ; shock is a transitory cost-push disturbance (an oil spike, a supply-chain disruption) that lifts inflation at a fixed Yₙ and then fades. Keeping them apart lets the user simulate a lasting structural shift and a passing supply shock independently.

6.5 The α slider, and the two meanings of a “steep” Phillips curve

α is exposed as a slider with default 0.3 (range 0.05–0.5). This is the empirically supported range for the Phillips-curve slope — not for the wage-setting α, which is a different and much larger number. Careful cross-sectional estimates put the slope around 0.3 (Hazell, Herreño, Nakamura & Steinsson, QJE 2022, find a 1-point rise in unemployment lowers inflation by about 0.34 points, and that the slope was small even in the early 1980s); recent US price-Phillips-curve estimates sit around 0.18–0.26 in normal labour markets. A slope near 1.0 is not empirically supported, which is why the slider is capped at 0.5.

Why two alphas at all? Blanchard's single-α model cannot fit both facts it is asked to fit. The natural-rate formula uₙ = (m + z)/α needs α ≈ 3 to deliver a plausible natural rate: with the tool's baseline m + z = 0.15, an α of 0.3 would imply uₙ = 0.15/0.3 = 50% unemployment. But the estimated Phillips-curve slope is ≈ 0.3, ten times smaller. One symbol is being asked to do two irreconcilable empirical jobs. The standard resolution — and the tool's — is that price stickiness and real rigidities flatten the observed inflation–output relation far below the underlying wage-setting sensitivity, so the two coefficients are genuinely different objects. The engine therefore keeps Blanchard's α in the natural-rate formula (as the fixed α_WS = 3.0) and gives the Phillips-curve slope its own slider. This is a deliberate deviation from the textbook, where they are one number.

Crucially, this matches Blanchard's own treatment: across the historical mutations of the Phillips curve he holds α fixed and varies θ, the expectations parameter, not α. Eqs. (8.5)→(8.8)→(8.9) walk through this with the same α throughout: at θ = 0 the original level trade-off (eq. 8.6); at 0 < θ < 1 inflation depends on both the gap and last year's inflation (eq. 8.8); at θ = 1 the accelerationist form πₜ − πₜ₋₁ = −α(uₜ − uₙ) (eq. 8.9), where the gap drives the change in inflation. Blanchard's Figure 8-4 plots estimated θ over time: it rose from the mid-1960s, stayed near 1 until the late 1990s, then fell back toward zero by 2020. The apparent steepening and flattening of the curve over decades is therefore a θ story, not an α story.

A clarification the tool makes deliberately, because two different things get called “steepness,” and neither of them is what the θ slider (shown as Max credibility) controls. The slope of the plotted Phillips curve — its tilt in (π, Y) space — is α/Yₙ, and only the α slider changes it. The curve's height is set by expected inflation πᵉ, so a given πᵉ fixes where the line sits at that moment.

θ does not move the plotted line on its own. Within a single frame it does nothing: the curve is drawn from whatever πᵉ currently is. θ only takes effect when you step the model forward one period, where it sets how πᵉ updates:

πᵉ′ = πᵉ + speed · [ A·(π − πᵉ) + (1 − A)·(π − πᵉ) ],  A = (1 − θ)·credibility

At A ≈ 1 (θ ≈ 0, anchored, full credibility) πᵉ is pulled back toward the 2% target each period; at A ≈ 0 (de-anchored) it is pulled toward last period's actual inflation, which is the spiral. So θ governs the speed and direction of the curve's vertical drift over successive periods — not its slope, and not an instantaneous jump. You only see its effect by stepping.

This is exactly why the “steepness” of Blanchard's Figure 8-4 is a different object again: it is the apparent slope of the inflation–unemployment scatter observed over many years. When expectations de-anchor, each period's inflation feeds more of itself into the next (the equation above with A → 0 — high θ or low credibility), and the cloud of points traces out a steeper — ultimately vertical — relationship over time. That is an emergent property of the multi-period drift, not the slope of the line on screen at any one moment.

So the 1970s “slope-looks-like-1” steepness is reproduced in the tool by de-anchoring expectations (raising θ, or letting credibility erode, so A → 0) and running the model forward, not by raising α and not by any single-frame change — faithful to Blanchard and the empirical evidence, which attribute that steepness to de-anchored expectations rather than a larger structural slope. Blanchard's own analytic Phillips curve keeps α as the slope at every θ; the accelerationist “vertical” curve is a statement about the long-run dynamic relationship, which is why the tool does not (and should not) rotate the plotted line when θ moves. And because the slope is α while the natural rate uses the separate fixed α_WS, experimenting with flat versus steep curves here never accidentally relocates potential output — that protection is the point of keeping the two apart.

6.7 Supply shocks: two distinct channels — Section 8-3

π = πᵉ + (α / Yₙ)(Y − Yₙ) + shock

StatusTOOL ADDITION
ChangeA decaying transitory pulse, so supply shocks shift the PC without moving uₙ or Yₙ.

Blanchard's own eq. 9.3 carries no shock term; the additive shock is the tool's addition, and the symbol z is reserved throughout for the structural wage-setting term of eq. 8.4.

Blanchard's Section 8-3 (the Covid and 1970s episodes) establishes that supply shocks come in two forms, and the tool models both:

  • Transitory cost-push pulse (the oil / Covid shock button). Blanchard finds the 2020–22 inflation came largely through the markup m — energy prices and supply-chain shortages raised firms' costs given wages — but that it faded: “when commodity prices started turning around, so did inflation.” This is a temporary m spike that decays (the tool's existing pulse with decay), lifting the PC while active but leaving the natural rate unchanged.
  • Permanent structural shift (the m and z sliders). A lasting change in market power (m, e.g., increased monopoly power) or wage-setting institutions (z, e.g., increased union strength) moves the natural rate via eq. (8.4) and therefore relocates potential output Yₙ. This is the channel the rebalancing scenario uses, and it persists until changed.

The de-anchoring link ties the two together: Blanchard notes the 1970s oil shock de-anchored expectations (θ rose), amplifying a transitory shock into sustained ~17% inflation, whereas the Covid shock did not de-anchor (credibility held) and so faded. With the tool's credibility slider, the same cost-push pulse can be run both ways: credibility high → fades (Covid-like); credibility low / de-anchoring on → spirals (1970s-like). The spiral is the accelerationist regime of eq. (8.9): when A → 0 (θ → 1) the gap drives the change in inflation, so inflation rises period-on-period rather than settling at a higher level. This is a behaviour to verify when the dynamics are tested.

7 The open-economy block (exchange rate and foreign output)

Y = Yₙ + [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁)] · [ (G−G₀) − c₁(T−T₀) − d₂(r−r₀) − n₁(ε−1) + x₁(Y*−Y₀) ]

Block 4 (open-economy stage only). Blanchard's Chapter 19 adds the exchange rate to the IS-LM model: net exports depend on the real exchange rate and on foreign output, and the exchange rate itself is pinned by uncovered interest parity. This block replaces the tool's ad hoc −100·(ε−1) term with a coefficient derived from eq. (19.2), and adds a foreign-output channel.

7.1 Uncovered interest parity — Chapters 17 and 19

Y = Yₙ + [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁)] · [ (G−G₀) − c₁(T−T₀) − d₂(r−r₀) − n₁(ε−1) + x₁(Y*−Y₀) ]

ToolE = Eᵉ(1 + i)/(1 + i*)
BlanchardE = Eᵉ(1 + i)/(1 + i*)  (eq. 19.5)
StatusEXACT

Investors arbitrage between domestic and foreign bonds, equalising expected returns. This is the uncovered interest parity (UIP) condition:

(1 + i) = (1 + i*) · (E / Eᵉ)
(eq. 17.2 / 19.3)

Solving for the current exchange rate, with the expected future rate Eᵉ taken as given:

E = Eᵉ · (1 + i) / (1 + i)
(eq. 19.5)

The tool's UIP relation E = Eᵉ·(1+i)/(1+i) is verbatim eq. (19.5) — the cleanest match in the model, no discrepancy. A higher domestic rate appreciates the currency (E rises); a higher foreign rate depreciates it. The approximate form (eq. 17.4), i ≈ i − (Eᵉ − E)/E, is the same relation: the domestic rate equals the foreign rate minus the expected appreciation.

UIP fixes the nominal exchange rate E, but the goods market responds to the real exchange rate ε — the relative price of foreign goods in common units. The two are linked by the domestic and foreign price levels:

ε = E · P / P*
(Ch. 17)

This is the step that closes the loop into the IS curve: UIP sets E from the interest-rate differential, the price-level ratio P/P* converts it to the real rate ε, and ε then enters net exports through the n₁(ε − 1) term in §7.2. In Blanchard's short-run simplification (eq. 19.2, P = P* = 1) the two coincide, ε = E; the tool keeps them distinct and lets both price levels evolve, which is what makes inflation differentials move the real exchange rate — the full dynamics of that conversion are set out in §7.8.

7.2 The open-economy IS with net exports — eq. 19.1 / 19.2

Y = Yₙ + [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁)] · [ (G−G₀) − c₁(T−T₀) − d₂(r−r₀) − n₁(ε−1) + x₁(Y*−Y₀) ]

ToolY = C + I + G + NX
BlanchardY = C(Y − T) + I(Y, r) + G + NX(Y, Y*, ε)  (eq. 19.1)
StatusEXACT
ChangeBlanchard’s eq. 19.2 simplification (P = P* = 1, r = i) applies only in his short run; the tool relaxes it — see §7.8.

Net exports are exports minus the value of imports, decreasing in the real exchange rate:

NX(Y, Y*, ε) = X(Y*, ε) − IM(Y, ε)/ε
(eq. 19.1)

The open-economy goods-market equilibrium adds net exports to the closed IS:

Y = C(Y−T) + I(Y, r) + G + NX(Y, Y*, ε)
(eq. 19.1)

Blanchard then simplifies (eq. 19.2): choosing P = P* = 1 so that ε = E (real and nominal exchange rates coincide), and — since the price level is fixed in the short run — r = i. The baseline is ε = 1, matching the tool's centring of the exchange-rate term on (ε − 1).

7.3 Linearising net exports (Marshall–Lerner)

Y = Yₙ + [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁)] · [ (G−G₀) − c₁(T−T₀) − d₂(r−r₀) − n₁(ε−1) + x₁(Y*−Y₀) ]

ToolNX = x₁Y* − m₁Y − n₁(ε − 1)
BlanchardNX(Y, Y*, ε) = X(Y*, ε) − IM(Y, ε)/ε  (eq. 19.1)
StatusLINEARISED
ChangeLinear propensities per Ch. 18 Problem 7; Marshall–Lerner (§18-4 p. 384; derived in the Ch. 18 Appendix, p. 393) assumed to hold, so the whole ε-effect collapses to one coefficient n₁ > 0.

Start from Blanchard's exact definition, in which the value of imports is deflated by the real exchange rate — imports are measured in foreign goods, so dividing by ε converts them to domestic-good units:

NX(Y, Y*, ε) = X(Y*, ε) − IM(Y, ε) / ε
(eq. 19.1)

The tool linearises this around the baseline ε = 1 (and X, IM around their baseline volumes). The key move is a first-order Taylor expansion in ε of the whole NX function, evaluated at ε = 1. Collect the three channels through which ε moves net exports:

  • Export volume: a real appreciation (ε up) makes home goods dearer abroad, so X falls: ∂X/∂ε < 0.
  • Import volume: a real appreciation makes foreign goods cheaper at home, so the quantity imported IM rises: ∂IM/∂ε > 0. This raises the import bill.
  • Valuation (the ÷ε term): the same appreciation cheapens each imported good in domestic-good units — the 1/ε factor falls as ε rises — which lowers the import bill. Differentiating IM/ε gives ∂(IM/ε)/∂ε = (∂IM/∂ε)/ε − IM/ε², so at ε = 1 this term is ∂IM/∂ε − IM: the volume effect minus the valuation effect.

Adding the export and import derivatives, the total derivative of NX with respect to ε at ε = 1 is

dNX/dε |ε=1 = ∂X/∂ε − ( ∂IM/∂ε − IM ) ≡ −n₁
(definition of n₁)

So the /ε has not disappeared — it is inside n₁. The single coefficient n₁ bundles all three channels: the fall in exports, the rise in import volume, and the offsetting valuation gain from the ÷ε term. These are precisely the three channels Blanchard identifies in §18-4 (p. 384), and Marshall–Lerner is his condition that they sum to a net improvement: in the formal derivation (Ch. 18 Appendix, “Derivation of the Marshall–Lerner Condition”, p. 393) he states it as the requirement that the sum of the three terms be positive. Equivalently here: the two volume effects together outweigh the valuation effect, so the bracket is positive and n₁ > 0 (a real appreciation worsens NX; a real depreciation improves it). Blanchard notes the condition is satisfied empirically and assumes it throughout, as does the tool. A first-order expansion then writes the ε-dependence as a straight line through the baseline:

NX ≈ NX|ε=1 + (dNX/dε)(ε − 1) = (x₁ Y* − m₁ Y) − n₁ (ε − 1)
(tool reduced form)

where the baseline balance is X − IM = x₁ Y* − m₁ Y (at ε = 1 the ÷ε factor is exactly 1, so the import bill equals the import volume), and the entire ε-response is carried by the −n₁(ε − 1) term. This is why the tool's NX shows no explicit division by ε: the linearisation has already folded the valuation effect of ÷ε into the constant n₁.

In this linearisation n₁ is a constant: the response of net exports to ε does not vary with the level of ε. The J-curve (net exports worsening before improving after a depreciation, because volumes adjust more slowly than prices) and any elasticity-dependence of the response are dynamic effects outside the scope of this static linear term; they are not modelled. What is retained is the sign and the constant magnitude, which is what eq. (19.2) requires under Marshall–Lerner.

7.4 Deriving the reduced-form coefficients

Y = Yₙ + [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁)] · [ (G−G₀) − c₁(T−T₀) − d₂(r−r₀) − n₁(ε−1) + x₁(Y*−Y₀) ]

Substitute the linear net-export terms into the open IS (eq. 19.2), retaining the investment accelerator (§2.2) and with x = 0:

Y = c₀ + c₁(Y−T) + d₀ + d₁Y − d₂ r + G + x₁Y* − m₁Y − n₁(ε−1)

Collect the Y terms. The import term −m₁Y now joins the left side, adding to the leakage:

(1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁) Y = [autonomous] − n₁(ε−1) + x₁Y*

Divide through. The open multiplier carries the extra +m₁ leakage in its denominator:

k_open = 1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁)

Because m₁ > 0, k_open is smaller than the closed multiplier 1/(1−c₁−d₁): imports leak out of the multiplier, the algebraic counterpart of Blanchard's “AA is flatter than DD” (Ch. 18). Every demand term — G, T, r, ε, and Y* — passes through this same k_open. The full open IS is:

Y = Yₙ + k_open·(G−G₀) − c₁·k_open·(T−T₀) − d₂·k_open·(r−r₀) − n₁·k_open·(ε−1) + x₁·k_open·(Y*−Y₀)

The reduced-form exchange-rate coefficient is therefore ∂Y/∂ε = −n₁·k_open, exactly parallel to the real-rate coefficient ∂Y/∂r = −d₂·k_open. Like d₂, the value of n₁ is calibration: Blanchard gives only its sign (Marshall–Lerner), no magnitude. The recommended documentation move is identical to the r coefficient — surface n₁ as the structural primitive and present the reduced-form ε coefficient as −n₁·k_open, rather than asserting a free-standing number.

7.6 The foreign-output channel

Y = Yₙ + [1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁)] · [ (G−G₀) − c₁(T−T₀) − d₂(r−r₀) − n₁(ε−1) + x₁(Y*−Y₀) ]

The term +x₁·k_open·(Y* − Y₀) gives the spillover from foreign output. A fall in foreign output Y* (a foreign recession) reduces domestic exports, cutting demand, amplified through the open multiplier. At x₁ = 0.3 and k_open = 1.43, a unit drop in Y* lowers domestic output by 0.43. Y* is exposed as a control so a student can run a foreign-recession spillover directly.

This unlocks a genuine three-channel external-shock lesson: a foreign recession (Y* down) and a real appreciation (ε up) both reduce net exports and can be compared as substitute sources of an external demand shock; and monetary policy can respond — cutting i depreciates E via UIP (§7.1), partially offsetting a loss of foreign demand. This is the cross-block interaction (goods market, interest rate, exchange rate) that Chapter 19 is built around.

7.8 The Real Exchange Rate and Price Evolution

Toolε = E·P/P* ;   P′ = P(1 + π·speed) ;   P*′ = P*(1 + π·speed)
Blanchardε = EP/P* (Ch. 17); Ch. 19 then sets P = P* = 1 so that ε = E
StatusTOOL ADDITION
ChangeBoth price levels evolve over time, so ε and E diverge; foreign inflation is held at the 2% target throughout.

The real exchange rate is defined as ε = E·P/P* (Blanchard Ch. 17, appreciation = ε up). The tool goes beyond the Ch. 19 simplification of fixing both price levels at 1: each step, the domestic price level grows at actual domestic inflation, P′ = P(1 + π·speed), while the foreign price level grows at a constant 2%, P*′ = P*(1 + π·speed) — the foreign central bank is treated as permanently on target.

Real appreciation and depreciation are therefore driven by the inflation differential (π − 2%). When domestic inflation runs above 2%, P outpaces P*, ε rises (real appreciation) and net exports fall through the n₁ channel of §7.3 — the mechanism behind the fiscal-expansion presets and the divergence between the ε and E lines on the UIP chart. When π = 2%, both price levels grow in lockstep and ε moves only with the nominal rate E.
