A guide for lecturers and seminar leaders · Blanchard, Macroeconomics 9th Global Edition

Economo is a single-file, browser-based simulator for teaching short-run and medium-run macroeconomics. It turns the core IS-LM-PC framework into something students manipulate directly: drag the curves, step through time, and watch the whole economy respond at once.

1About this tool

Economo is one progressive learning tool, not a set of separate models. The full IS-LM-PC-UIP system — closed and open economy, goods market through Phillips curve — lives in a single HTML file. The whole model is always on screen; the blocks you have not yet taught are greyed out and locked. As you move through the stages, complexity unlocks in the order your syllabus follows.

It runs in any desktop browser (the HTML isn't built for small mobile screens) with no install, no login, and no internet connection. Project it in a lecture, embed it in your VLE, or email the file to students.

2The four teaching stages

A dropdown in the top bar (with a Next button beside it) moves between stages. Each stage unlocks one more block of the model. The order follows the University of Sussex course syllabus, not Blanchard's chapter order — the open economy is introduced before the Phillips curve.

StageModelEconomyWhat unlocksRate regime
1IS-LMClosedGoods market, IS-LM chart, policy rater = i — prices fixed, no inflation
2IS-LM-UIPOpen+ Imports/exports, UIP, exchange ratestill r = i
3IS-LM-PCClosed+ Phillips curve, expectations, Yn, Okunr = i − πᵉ switches on
4Full ModelOpenEverything combinedr = i − πᵉ
The key pedagogical move
The nominal-versus-real distinction does not exist until the Phillips curve is unlocked. In stages 1 and 2 the economy is Blanchard's core short run: prices are constant, so the real rate equals the nominal rate (r = i), and there is no expected-inflation control. The Fisher line, the real-rate line, and the πᵉ slider all appear together the moment the PC block unlocks at stage 3. This keeps the early stages honest — students are not asked to reason about real rates before they have met inflation.

The two gates behind the four stages. These are not four models. They are the four combinations of two switches inside one engine. The open/closed gate adds or removes the trade terms x₁Y* − n₁(ε−1) and the UIP panel; it oscillates across the stages (off, on, off, on), which is why the exchange rate appears, disappears, and returns. The inflation gate decides whether the engine computes r = i or r = i − πᵉ; it latches on at stage 3 and stays on. Every stage transition is one of these two switches moving.

3The interface at a glance

The top bar

The top bar carries the stage dropdown and Next button, the period counter, and the run controls.

Help mode

Click the Help Mode button in the top bar to toggle it on. When active, hovering over any symbol on the charts or equations will display its definition.

Step controls

Step one period advances the economy one period so a shock can play out; Reverse steps back through the history (it shows how many steps are stored); and Run to rebalance runs the model forward until it reaches the medium-run equilibrium. The controls reset cleanly when you load a new scenario.

Copy state

Click the Copy State button to copy the current values of all variables to your clipboard. You can paste this into a spreadsheet or text document.

The sidebar

The left sidebar holds every control, grouped into collapsible sections by chart:

  • Preset scenarios — a dropdown, a Load button, and a description box.
  • IS-LM — the main goods-market and monetary policy levers (G, T, policy rate i, Taylor response φ, MPC c₁).
  • UIP curve (FX) — open-economy variables (foreign rate i*, expected exchange rate Eᵉ, import propensity m₁, foreign output Y*).
  • Phillips curve & supply side — expectations anchoring (Max credibility (1 − θ) and Current credibility), expected inflation πᵉ, Phillips curve slope α, structural markup m and wage-push z, and the transitory cost-push shock.

Every slider has an editable value beside it: drag it, or click the number and type a value directly. Sliders for locked blocks are greyed until their stage unlocks.

4The three charts

Curves with a hollow circle on them are draggable. Dragging changes the underlying parameter and redraws everything at once — this is the feature that makes simultaneity visible.

ISLM — goods market & policy rate

The solid blue line is the nominal LM (the policy rate). The dashed blue line is the real rate r = i − πᵉ, which only appears once the PC block unlocks. Drag the IS curve for fiscal policy; drag the LM line for monetary policy. A faint dashed vertical marks natural output Yn (once the PC block unlocks).

UIP — interest rate & exchange rate

Solid green is the UIP relation in nominal terms, E = Eᵉ(1+i)/(1+i*). The gold dashed line is the real exchange rate ε = E·P/P*. The two diverge as home prices move relative to foreign.

Phillips curve

Drag the curve vertically to change expected inflation πᵉ. A transitory cost-push shock lifts the whole curve without moving Yn. The slope of the plotted line is α/Yn and only the α slider changes it — the credibility slider never tilts it, it only drives the curve's vertical drift when you step forward. The derivation drill-down expands the labour market (WS-PS), the Okun bridge from unemployment to output, and the Phillips relation itself.

5Controls and what they mean

  • Government spending G (10–35) and Taxes T (10–35). The fiscal levers. Baseline is a balanced budget at G = T = 20 (about a 20% government share of output).
  • Policy rate i (−2% to 15%). The monetary lever throughout — the central bank sets the nominal rate directly, giving a flat policy line. When the Taylor rule is on, this is overridden by the rule.
  • Taylor response φ (0–3). When the Taylor rule is on, the bank sets the rate by a smoothed Taylor rule. Values of φ above 1 satisfy the Taylor principle.
  • MPC c₁ (0.30–0.80). The marginal propensity to consume. Raising it steepens the multiplier; the live readout shows the updated multiplier and response coefficients.
  • Foreign rate i* (0–10%), Expected exchange rate Eᵉ (0.7–1.3), Import propensity m₁ (0.05–0.60), and Foreign output Y* (80–120). Open-economy variables.
  • Expected inflation πᵉ (−2% to 15%). Shifts the Phillips curve and drives the real rate r = i − πᵉ.
  • PC slope α (0.05–0.50, default 0.30). How strongly the output gap feeds into inflation. Calibrated against Hazell et al. (QJE 2022), who find a 1-point rise in unemployment lowers inflation by roughly 0.34 points; the slider is capped at 0.5 because a slope near 1.0 is not empirically supported. This is not the same α that sets the natural rate. The natural rate uses a separate, fixed wage-setting sensitivity α_WS = 3.0. Moving the PC slope never relocates Yn — see the two-alpha note in §6.
  • Markup m and Wage push z (structural). These shift natural output Yn through the wage-setting relation, not the Phillips curve directly. Distinct from the transitory cost-push shock.
  • Max credibility (1 − θ) and Current credibility. How firmly expectations stay anchored to the 2% target. The engine stores Blanchard's θ (eq. 8.7) with exactly his meaning: θ = 0 is fully anchored (πᵉ = target) and θ = 1 is fully adaptive (πᵉ = last period's inflation — the accelerationist spiral). The slider is displayed as its complement 1 − θ, so dragging right = more credible = more anchored, and so that it runs the same direction as the Current-credibility slider. That is a display choice only; the value entering every equation is Blanchard's θ. Higher credibility (lower θ) damps the inflation response to shocks. Note that θ does nothing within a single frame — it never tilts the plotted Phillips curve. It sets the speed and direction of the curve's vertical drift as you step forward, via πᵉ′ = πᵉ + speed·[ A(π̄ − πᵉ) + (1 − A)(π − πᵉ) ], with A = (1 − θ)·credibility.
  • Cost-push shock (PC shift) and the oil-shock button. A transitory supply disturbance that lifts the Phillips curve and decays over time. It is labelled shock, not z: the symbol z is reserved throughout for Blanchard's structural wage-push term in eq. (8.4), which moves the natural rate. The transitory shock does not.

6Equations and calibration

For the complete system of equations block by block, see the Model Textbook Correspondence.

Output is solved each period as a comparative-static equilibrium, then advanced dynamically. The closed and open economy share one engine — the closed economy is the open-economy IS with the import term forced to zero.

IS (goods market)

Y = C + I + G  (closed)→ Correspondence
Y = C + I + G + NX  (open)→ Correspondence
C = c₀ + c₁(Y − T)→ Correspondence
I = d₀ + d₁Y − d₂r→ Correspondence
NX = x₁Y* − m₁Y − n₁(ε−1)→ Correspondence

In the UI's equation panel, the model solves for equilibrium output Y and displays the sum of its components (C + I + G + NX) to students so they can intuitively trace exactly which sector of the economy is responding to a shock.

Under the hood, the engine calculates this instantaneously using the reduced-form multiplier equation:

Closed:   k = 1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁) = 2.5  at baseline (c₁=0.5, d₁=0.1)→ Correspondence
Open:     k_o = 1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁) ≈ 1.43  at baseline (+ m₁=0.3)→ Correspondence

The import leakage m₁ sits inside the denominator, so the open multiplier is smaller and the open IS curve is steeper than the closed one. Baseline reduced-form responses (closed): ΔY = +2.5 ΔG, −1.25 ΔT, −500 Δr.

LM / MP (monetary policy)

r = i  (stages 1–2, prices fixed)  →  r = i − πᵉ  (stage 3 onward)→ Correspondence

Following Blanchard's modern treatment (Ch. 5 and Ch. 9), money supply and money demand are removed entirely: the central bank sets the nominal rate i directly, so the LM curve is a horizontal MP line and the money stock is endogenous.

Neutral nominal rate:   ī = rₙ + π̄  (= 3% at baseline)→ Correspondence
Taylor rule (when on):   i = ρ·i₋₁ + (1 − ρ)·( ī + φ(π − π̄) + ψ(Y − Yₙ)/Yₙ ),   ρ = 0.75, ψ = 0.25→ Correspondence

Blanchard's Ch. 23 rule is written in the unemployment gap, i = ī + a(π − π̄) − b(u − uₙ). The tool rewrites it in the output gap using the identity (u − uₙ) = −(Y − Yₙ)/L, which flips the sign: the tool's positive coefficient ψ is Blanchard's negative −b after that conversion, not a disagreement. Two further points are deliberate departures from the textbook rule and are documented as such: a and b are renamed φ and ψ, and the tool adds interest smoothing (ρ = 0.75) — the rate adjusts partially toward its rule-implied target each period. Blanchard's stated rule has no lagged-rate term; smoothing is retained here because without it a bank reacting instantly to current gaps can trigger severe period-to-period oscillations that would destabilise the simulation. φ above 1 is the Taylor principle.

UIP (exchange rate)

E = Eᵉ(1+i)/(1+i*)→ Correspondence
ε = E·P/P*  ;  P′ = P(1 + π·speed)  ;  P*′ = P*(1 + π̄·speed)→ Correspondence

UIP fixes the nominal exchange rate E and is a verbatim match to Blanchard's eq. (19.5) — the cleanest correspondence in the model. But the goods market responds to the real exchange rate ε, and here the tool goes beyond Blanchard's Ch. 19 simplification of fixing P = P* = 1 (so that ε = E). Both price levels evolve: the domestic level grows at actual inflation π, the foreign level at a constant 2% (the foreign central bank is treated as permanently on target). Real appreciation and depreciation are therefore driven by the inflation differential (π − 2%), which is why the ε and E lines diverge on the UIP chart.

PC (Phillips curve) — gap form

π = πᵉ + α(Y − Yn)/Yn + shock→ Correspondence
un = (m + zstruct) / α_WS  ;  Yn = L(1 − un)→ Correspondence
Why structural shocks show nothing in the PC line
The Phillips curve is written in gap form. The structural markup m and structural wage-push zstruct live inside natural output Yn via the wage-setting relation, so moving those sliders shifts Yn rather than appearing as a term in the PC equation. The "+ shock" in the PC line is a separate transitory cost-push disturbance that sits at zero at rest and decays after a pulse. It is called shock and not z precisely so that z can stay reserved for Blanchard's structural term. The two are deliberately kept apart: zstruct is a permanent wage-setting pressure that moves Yn; shock lifts inflation at a fixed Yn and then fades.
The two alphas — a deliberate deviation from the textbook
In Blanchard, the α in the Phillips curve and the α in the natural-rate formula un = (m + z)/α are the same number. In the tool they deliberately are not, and this is the one genuine DEVIATION in the PC block. The reason is that a single α cannot fit both facts it is asked to fit. The natural-rate formula needs α ≈ 3 to give a plausible un: at the baseline m + z = 0.15, an α of 0.3 would imply 50% unemployment. But the empirically estimated Phillips-curve slope is around 0.3 — ten times smaller. The engine therefore keeps Blanchard's α in the natural-rate formula as a fixed structural constant α_WS = 3.0, and gives the Phillips-curve slope its own slider α (default 0.3). Price stickiness and real rigidities flatten the observed inflation–output relation far below the underlying wage-setting sensitivity, so the two are genuinely different objects. A practical benefit: experimenting with flat versus steep Phillips curves can never accidentally relocate potential output.
Expectations: what θ does and does not do
πᵉ′ = πᵉ + speed·[ A(π̄ − πᵉ) + (1 − A)(π − πᵉ) ],   A = (1 − θ)·credibility
At credibility = 1 this reduces to Blanchard's eq. (8.7), πᵉ = (1 − θ)π̄ + θπ₋₁, exactly; the endogenous credibility stock is the tool's one addition. Blanchard holds α fixed and varies θ across the historical mutations of the Phillips curve (eqs. 8.5 → 8.8 → 8.9), and the tool follows him. So the apparent steepening of the 1970s Phillips curve is a θ story, not an α story: it is reproduced by de-anchoring expectations and running the model forward, not by raising α and not by any single-frame change. θ never rotates the plotted line — it governs the line's vertical drift over successive periods.

7Preset scenarios

Six preset scenarios load a parameter set and a teaching narrative. Use them as lecture set-pieces or seminar prompts.

A note on how credibility is quoted below
Each scenario is described using the value on the slider you actually see, Max credibility (1 − θ). Higher = more anchored. Where the underlying Blanchard parameter θ matters for the economics, it is given alongside. Recall the engine convention: θ = 0 is fully anchored, θ = 1 is fully adaptive.
  1. 1. The Taylor Principle — Taylor rule ON with φ = 1.5 and only partially anchored expectations (Max credibility ≈ 0.25, i.e. θ ≈ 0.75), so expectations can actually move. Apply the oil shock and step: with φ = 1.5 the bank responds more than one-for-one and inflation comes smoothly back to the 2% target within the medium run. Then—without resetting—lower φ to 0.5 and shock again. Now the bank is too passive: the rate is cut to the ZLB floor and pinned there for several periods, expectations de-anchor (watch the warning chip), and the return to target takes far longer and oscillates. The contrast between the two φ settings is the Taylor principle.
  2. 2a. De-Anchored Expectations (1970s) — Twin experiment, part 1 — identical to preset 2b in every field except the credibility setting. Max credibility ≈ 0.15 (θ ≈ 0.85): expectations are mostly adaptive, so πᵉ chases whatever inflation actually does. A transitory cost-push shock of +5% hits: inflation spikes to 7% and πᵉ follows it upward. The central bank now fights a moving target: rates go higher for longer, inflation stays elevated for roughly twice as long as in 2b, and the cumulative output loss is about 1.5× larger.
  3. 2b. Anchored Expectations (1990s) — Twin experiment, part 2 — identical to preset 2a in every field except the credibility setting. Max credibility = 1 (θ = 0): credibility is absolute and πᵉ stays nailed to the 2% target no matter what inflation does. The same +5% transitory cost-push shock produces a sharper but brief slump: one decisive hike, inflation is back near target within a couple of periods, and the episode is over by around t = 12. Central-bank credibility is itself a policy tool.
  4. 3. Exchange-Rate Disinflation — Taylor OFF, expectations mostly anchored (Max credibility ≈ 0.7, i.e. θ ≈ 0.3). Cut the policy rate to 1%. UIP triggers a nominal depreciation; net exports surge and Y rises above Yₙ. Over time prices climb relative to foreign, the real exchange rate appreciates, and Y drifts back.
  5. 4. Twin Deficits & Real Appreciation — Taylor OFF, expectations mostly anchored (Max credibility ≈ 0.7, i.e. θ ≈ 0.3). A modest fiscal expansion (G raised from 20 to 22) with no monetary offset drives up domestic prices. The real exchange rate appreciates, crowding out net exports until Y returns to Yₙ and inflation returns to 2% — leaving ε permanently higher.
  6. 5. Global Rate Hike Spillover — Foreign rates jump to 6%. UIP wants a weaker domestic currency unless the CB matches the hike. With Taylor ON, the domestic rule sees the inflationary depreciation and hikes i. Because the rule anchors to the structurally computed neutral rate, the economy converges cleanly: i settles at the new world rate (6%).

8Teaching with Economo

  • Start locked. Open at stage 1 so students see the full model greyed out — the destination is visible from the first lecture, which motivates each unlock.
  • Drag before you derive. Let students predict the direction of a shift, then drag the curve to test it. The live equation panel under each chart confirms the algebra.
  • Step through dynamics. Equilibrium comparison is one lesson; watching prices adjust period by period back to potential is another. Use Step / Reverse to slow it down.
  • Pair the scenarios. Scenarios 2a and 2b are designed as a contrast — same shock, different credibility. The side-by-side is the lesson.

9FAQ & troubleshooting

Why does closed-economy output settle at Y = 90, not 100?

In stages 1–2 the engine runs r = i with no inflation (Blanchard's fixed-price short run). At the baseline policy rate of 3%, the real rate is the full 3%, borrowing is expensive, investment is low, and the goods market clears at Y = 90 — below potential Yn = 100. This is a real-rate result, not a multiplier artefact, and not a sign the closed economy is "smaller." Once the PC block unlocks at stage 3, inflation of 2% opens the Fisher wedge (r = 3% − 2% = 1%), the lower real rate spurs investment, and output returns to potential.

A permanent open-economy supply shock never settles.

The open economy uses backward-looking exchange-rate expectations, so permanent supply shocks have no stationary equilibrium under UIP. Use transitory shocks for open-economy work; forward-looking FX dynamics are out of scope for this version.

Nothing installs — is that right?

Yes. It is one HTML file. Double-click it, or host it on your VLE. No IT involvement, works offline.
