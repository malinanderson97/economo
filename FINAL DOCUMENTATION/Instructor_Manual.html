Economo
IS-LM-PC Interactive Macro Lab
A guide for lecturers and seminar leaders · Blanchard, Macroeconomics 9th Global Edition

Economo is a single-file, browser-based simulator for teaching short-run and medium-run macroeconomics. It turns the core IS-LM-PC framework into something students manipulate directly: drag the curves, step through time, and watch the whole economy respond at once.

1About this tool

Economo is one progressive learning tool, not a set of separate models. The full IS-LM-PC-UIP system — closed and open economy, goods market through Phillips curve — lives in a single HTML file. The whole model is always on screen; the blocks you have not yet taught are greyed out and locked. As you move through the stages, complexity unlocks in the order your syllabus follows.

It runs in any desktop browser (the HTML isn't built for small mobile screens) with no install, no login, and no internet connection. Project it in a lecture, embed it in your VLE, or email the file to students.

2The four teaching stages

A dropdown in the top bar (with a Next button beside it) moves between stages. Each stage unlocks one more block of the model. The order follows the course syllabus, not Blanchard's chapter order — the open economy is introduced before the Phillips curve.

The key pedagogical move
The nominal-versus-real distinction does not exist until the Phillips curve is unlocked. In stages 0 and 1 the economy is Blanchard's core short run: prices are constant, so the real rate equals the nominal rate (r = i), and there is no expected-inflation control. The Fisher line, the real-rate line, and the πᵉ slider all appear together the moment the PC block unlocks at stage 2. This keeps the early stages honest — students are not asked to reason about real rates before they have met inflation.

3The interface at a glance

The top bar

The top bar carries the stage dropdown and Next button, the period counter, and the run controls: Step one period advances the economy one period so a shock can play out; Reverse steps back through the history (it shows how many steps are stored); and the controls reset cleanly when you load a new scenario.

The sidebar

The left sidebar holds every control, grouped into collapsible sections:

  • Preset scenarios — a dropdown, a Load button, and a description box.
  • Policy & exogenous variables — the main sliders: G, T, the policy rate i, and (once unlocked) the open-economy variables.
  • Adjustment dynamics — price-flexibility (medium-run speed), expectations anchoring (θ), allow de-anchoring toggle, and the Taylor rule.
  • Supply shocks — the structural markup, structural wage-push, and a transitory cost-push shock slider.

Every slider has an editable value beside it: drag it, or click the number and type a value directly. Sliders for locked blocks are greyed until their stage unlocks.

Help mode

Click the Help Mode button at the bottom of the sidebar to toggle it on. When active, hovering over any symbol on the charts or equations will display its definition.

4The three charts

Curves with a hollow circle on them are draggable. Dragging changes the underlying parameter and redraws everything at once — this is the feature that makes simultaneity visible.

ISLM — goods market & policy rate

The solid blue line is the nominal LM (the policy rate). The dashed blue line is the real rate r = i − πᵉ, which only appears once the PC block unlocks. Drag the IS curve for fiscal policy; drag the LM line for monetary policy. A faint dashed vertical marks natural output Yn (once the PC block unlocks).

UIP — interest rate & exchange rate

Solid green is the UIP relation in nominal terms, E = Eᵉ(1+i)/(1+i*). The gold dashed line is the real exchange rate ε = E·P/P*. The two diverge as home prices move relative to foreign. This chart is live from stage 1 onward.

Phillips curve

Drag the curve vertically to change expected inflation πᵉ. A cost-push shock z lifts the whole curve. The derivation drill-down expands the labour market (WS-PS), the Okun bridge from unemployment to output, and the Phillips relation itself. Live from stage 2.

5Controls and what they mean

  • Government spending G (10–35) and Taxes T (10–35). The fiscal levers. Baseline is a balanced budget at G = T = 20 (about a 20% government share of output).
  • Policy rate i (−2% to 15%). The monetary lever throughout — the central bank sets the nominal rate directly, giving a flat policy line. When the Taylor rule is on, this is overridden by the rule.
  • MPC c₁ (0.30–0.80). The marginal propensity to consume. Raising it steepens the multiplier; the live readout shows the updated multiplier and response coefficients.
  • Expected inflation πᵉ (−2% to 15%). Unlocks at stage 2. Shifts the Phillips curve and drives the real rate r = i − πᵉ.
  • PC slope α (0.05–0.50, default 0.30). How strongly the output gap feeds into inflation. Calibrated against Hazell et al. (QJE 2022).
  • Markup m and Wage push z (structural). These shift natural output Yn through the wage-setting relation, not the Phillips curve directly. Distinct from the transitory cost-push shock.
  • Import propensity m₁ (0.05–0.60), Foreign output Y*, Foreign rate i*, Expected exchange rate Eᵉ. Open-economy, from stage 1.
  • Anchoring ceiling θ (0–1) and credibility. How firmly expectations stay anchored to target. High θ damps the inflation response to shocks.
  • Taylor rule toggle and coefficient φ (0–3). When on, the bank sets the rate by a smoothed Taylor rule. Values of φ above 1 satisfy the Taylor principle.
  • Cost-push shock z and the oil-shock button. A transitory supply disturbance that lifts the Phillips curve and decays over time.

6Equations and calibration

Output is solved each period as a comparative-static equilibrium, then advanced dynamically. The closed and open economy share one engine — the closed economy is the open-economy IS with the import term forced to zero.

IS (goods market)

Closed:   k = 1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁) = 2.5  at baseline (c₁=0.5, d₁=0.1)
Open:     k_o = 1 / (1 − c₁ − d₁ + m₁) ≈ 1.43  at baseline (+ m₁=0.3)Blanchard eq. 19.1

The import leakage m₁ sits inside the denominator, so the open multiplier is smaller and the open IS curve is steeper than the closed one. Baseline reduced-form responses (closed): ΔY = +2.5 ΔG, −1.25 ΔT, −500 Δr.

MP (monetary policy)

r = i  (stages 0–1, prices fixed)  →  r = i − πᵉ  (stage 2 onward)

The central bank sets the nominal rate i directly. In the closed stage, money demand is multiplicative, M/P = Y·(L₀ − L₁·i); in the open stage it is abstracted out.

PC (Phillips curve) — gap form

π = πᵉ + α(Y − Yn)/Yn + zBlanchard eq. 9.3
un = (m + zstruct) / α_WS  ;  Yn = L(1 − un)eq. 8.4
Why structural shocks show nothing in the PC line
The Phillips curve is written in gap form. The structural markup m and structural wage-push zstruct live inside natural output Yn via the wage-setting relation, so moving those sliders shifts Yn rather than appearing as a term in the PC equation. The "+ z" in the PC line is a separate transitory cost-push shock that sits at zero at rest and decays after a pulse. The two are deliberately kept apart.

7Preset scenarios

Six preset scenarios load a parameter set and a teaching narrative. Use them as lecture set-pieces or seminar prompts.

  1. 1. The Taylor Principle — Taylor rule ON with φ = 1.5 and partially-anchored expectations (θ=0.25), so expectations can actually move. Apply the oil shock and step: with φ = 1.5 the bank responds more than one-for-one and inflation comes smoothly back to the 2% target within the medium run. Then—without resetting—lower φ to 0.5 and shock again. Now the bank is too passive: the rate is cut to the ZLB floor and pinned there for several periods, expectations de-anchor (watch the warning chip), and the return to target takes far longer and oscillates. The contrast between the two φ settings is the Taylor principle.
  2. 2a. De-Anchored Expectations (1970s) — Twin experiment, part 1 — identical to preset 2b in every field except θ. θ = 0.15: expectations are mostly adaptive. A transitory supply shock (z_pulse = +5%) hits: inflation spikes to 7% and πᵉ chases it upward. The central bank now fights a moving target: rates go higher for longer, inflation stays elevated for roughly twice as long as in 2b, and the cumulative output loss is about 1.5× larger.
  3. 2b. Anchored Expectations (1990s) — Twin experiment, part 2 — identical to preset 2a in every field except θ. θ = 1: credibility is absolute and πᵉ stays nailed to 2% no matter what inflation does. The same +5% transitory supply shock produces a sharper but brief slump: one decisive hike, inflation is back near target within a couple of periods, and the episode is over by around t = 12. Central-bank credibility is itself a policy tool.
  4. 3. Exchange-Rate Disinflation — Taylor OFF, expectations mostly anchored (θ=0.7). Cut the policy rate to 1%. UIP triggers a nominal depreciation; net exports surge and Y rises above Yₙ. Over time prices climb relative to foreign, the real exchange rate appreciates, and Y drifts back.
  5. 4. Twin Deficits & Real Appreciation — Taylor OFF, expectations mostly anchored (θ=0.7). A modest fiscal expansion (G raised from 20 to 22) with no monetary offset drives up domestic prices. The real exchange rate appreciates, crowding out net exports until Y returns to Yₙ and inflation returns to 2% — leaving ε permanently higher.
  6. 5. Global Rate Hike Spillover — Foreign rates jump to 6%. UIP wants a weaker domestic currency unless the CB matches the hike. With Taylor ON, the domestic rule sees the inflationary depreciation and hikes i. Because the rule anchors to the structurally computed neutral rate, the economy converges cleanly: i settles at the new world rate (6%).

8Teaching with Economo

  • Start locked. Open at stage 0 so students see the full model greyed out — the destination is visible from the first lecture, which motivates each unlock.
  • Drag before you derive. Let students predict the direction of a shift, then drag the curve to test it. The live equation panel under each chart confirms the algebra.
  • Step through dynamics. Equilibrium comparison is one lesson; watching prices adjust period by period back to potential is another. Use Step / Reverse to slow it down.
  • Pair the scenarios. Scenarios 2a and 2b are designed as a contrast — same shock, different credibility. The side-by-side is the lesson.

9FAQ & troubleshooting

Why does closed-economy output settle at Y = 90, not 100?

In stages 0–1 the engine runs r = i with no inflation (the fixed-price short run). At the baseline policy rate of 3%, the goods market clears at Y = 90, below potential Yn = 100. This is a real-rate result, not a sign the closed economy is "smaller." Output returns to 100 once the PC block unlocks and the real rate falls.

A permanent open-economy supply shock never settles.

The open economy uses backward-looking exchange-rate expectations, so permanent supply shocks have no stationary equilibrium under UIP. Use transitory shocks for open-economy work; forward-looking FX dynamics are out of scope for this version.

Nothing installs — is that right?

Yes. It is one HTML file. Double-click it, or host it on your VLE. No IT involvement, works offline.
