Taylor rule active
Allow expectations to de-anchor
π* = 2% target. Expectations are anchored by the product of two sliders: Max anchoring θ is the ceiling (how credible this central bank can ever be), and Current credibility is where anchoring sits right now (0–100% of that ceiling). Effective anchoring = θ × credibility: at θ=1, full credibility pins expectations to π*; at credibility 0, expectations adapt fully to past inflation.
Allow de-anchoring: when on, current credibility moves on its own each period — it erodes if inflation sits far from target for a sustained stretch, and rebuilds toward the ceiling when inflation returns to target. You can also drag credibility yourself at any time (e.g. start it low to model an economy with unmoored expectations, then run stable policy to earn it back). When off, credibility is held fixed where you set it.
With the Taylor rule ON the CB moves its rate toward neutral plus φ(π−π*) + 0.25·gap (φ > 1 is the Taylor principle).
Note: designed for comparative-static stepping, not multi-decade simulation.

▶Government debt
Set a starting debt ratio d = B/Y and a trend growth rate g, then create a primary deficit by raising G above T (or a surplus to pay debt down). The debt ratio evolves by Δd = (r − g)·d − s, where s = (T−G)/Y is the primary surplus. When r > g the ratio snowballs even with a balanced budget; when g > r it erodes on its own. g enters the debt accounting only — it is an assumed trend, not modelled growth in output.
