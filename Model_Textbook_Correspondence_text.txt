3.2 Why the tax multiplier is asymmetric
The tax multiplier ?c?/(1 ? c? ? d?) carries d? in the denominator but only c? in the numerator. This is not an inconsistency. A tax change acts on disposable income, and in this model only consumption responds to disposable income; investment responds to output (Y), not to (Y ? T). So the direct first-round impact of a tax rise runs through consumption alone (numerator ?c?), while the subsequent amplification runs through both consumption and the investment accelerator as output falls (shared denominator 1 ? c? ? d?).
By contrast a spending change hits demand directly and fully (numerator 1), which is why |?Y/?T| < ?Y/?G and the balanced-budget multiplier is non-zero.

5. The LM / MP block (money market and the policy rate)

6.6 Supply shocks: two distinct channels Ś Section 8-3
Blanchard's Section 8-3 (the Covid and 1970s episodes) establishes that supply shocks come in two forms, and the tool models both:
Ģ Transitory cost-push pulse (the oil / Covid shock button). Blanchard finds the 2020¢22 inflation came largely through the markup m Ś energy prices and supply-chain shortages raised firms' costs given wages Ś but that it faded: ōwhen commodity prices started turning around, so did inflation.ö This is a temporary m spike that decays (the tool's existing pulse with decay), lifting the PC while active but leaving the natural rate unchanged.
Ģ Permanent structural shift (the m and z sliders). A lasting change in market power (m) or wage-setting institutions (z) moves the natural rate via eq. (8.4) and therefore relocates potential output Y?. This is the channel the rebalancing scenario uses, and it persists until changed.
The de-anchoring link ties the two together: Blanchard notes the 1970s oil shock de-anchored expectations (? rose), amplifying a transitory shock into sustained ~17% inflation, whereas the Covid shock did not de-anchor (credibility held) and so faded. With the tool's credibility slider, the same cost-push pulse can be run both ways: credibility high ? fades (Covid-like); credibility low / de-anchoring on ? spirals (1970s-like). The spiral is the accelerationist regime of eq. (8.9): when ?_eff ? 1 the gap drives the change in inflation, so inflation rises period-on-period rather than settling at a higher level. This is a behaviour to verify when the dynamics are tested.

7.1 Uncovered interest parity Ś Chapters 17 and 19
Investors arbitrage between domestic and foreign bonds, equalising expected returns. This is the uncovered interest parity (UIP) condition:
(1 + i) = (1 + i*) Ę (E? / E)      (eq. 17.2 / 19.3)
Solving for the current exchange rate, with the expected future rate E? taken as given:

The tool's UIP relation E = E?Ę(1+i)/(1+i) is verbatim eq. (19.5) Ś the cleanest match in the model, no discrepancy. A higher domestic rate appreciates the currency (E rises); a higher foreign rate depreciates it. The approximate form (eq. 17.4), i ? i ? (E? ? 7.2 The open-economy IS with net exports Ś eq. 19.1 / 19.2
Net exports are exports minus the value of imports, decreasing in the real exchange rate:

7.3 Linearising net exports (Marshall¢Lerner)
Following Blanchard's own linear trade system (Ch. 18, Problem 7/8), linearise the components of net exports:
X = x? Y*        (exports rise with foreign output)
IM = m? Y       (imports rise with domestic output; ?=1 ? IM/? = IM)

Here n? > 0 is the sensitivity of net exports to the real exchange rate. The condition n? > 0 is precisely the Marshall¢Lerner condition, which Blanchard assumes holds throughout Chapter 19. Economically, a real appreciation has two opposing effects on net exports: it reduces export volumes and import volumes (quantity effect, lowering NX) but cheapens each imported good in domestic terms (valuation effect, raising NX). Marshall¢Lerner is the condition that the volume effects dominate, so that on net a real appreciation worsens net exports Ś i.e. n? > 0.
In this linearisation n? is a constant: the response of net exports to ? does not vary with the level of ?. The J-curve (net exports worsening before improving after a depreciation, because volumes adjust more slowly than prices) and any elasticity-dependence of the response are dynamic effects outside the scope of this static linear term; they are not modelled. What is retained is the sign and the constant magnitude, which is what eq. (19.2) requires under Marshall¢Lerner.
